🏢 Considerations by Company Size

Under 100 FTE
  • FMLA does not apply (threshold is 50+ employees)
  • State laws may still apply (CA, NY, WA etc.)
  • STD insurance is the most cost-effective way to fund leave
  • Set a simple, written policy even if modest — clarity reduces risk
  • Budget for backfill or coverage costs
  • Consider a "build over time" philosophy — start at 8 wks and grow
100–500 FTE
  • FMLA applies at 50+ employees — ensure compliance
  • Consider dedicated leave administration (Cocoon, Sparrow, etc.)
  • Aim for 12–16 wks birthing, 8–12 wks non-birthing
  • Coordinate STD + company top-up clearly in policy docs
  • Train managers on leave conversations and return-to-work
  • Review state-specific obligations as headcount grows
500+ FTE
  • Competitive bar is 16–20 wks birthing, 12 wks non-birthing
  • Dedicated leave ops or vendor essential at this scale
  • Audit equity across roles, geographies, and parental types
  • Return-to-work programs and phased re-entry are expected
  • Global considerations if operating internationally
  • Consider fertility, adoption, and loss benefits as part of suite

🗺️ State Law Nuances

🌴 California
  • SDI (State Disability Insurance): Covers pregnancy/birth — up to 52 weeks disability; typically 60–70% wage replacement (up to 1,620/wk in 2025)
  • CA PFL: Up to 8 weeks bonding leave at 60–70% wages (paid by state)
  • CFRA: Up to 12 weeks job-protected unpaid leave (applies at 5+ employees in CA)
  • PDL: Pregnancy disability leave — up to 4 months, separate from CFRA
  • Employer policy stacks on top of state benefits
🌉 San Francisco (PPLO)
  • SF PPLO requires employers with 20+ employees to supplement CA PFL to 100% of salary
  • Cap: $2,700/week employer top-up
  • Applies to bonding leave (not SDI disability period)
  • Duration: up to 8 weeks of supplementation
  • Runs concurrently with CA PFL — employer pays the gap
  • Applies to employees working in SF, not just HQ'd there
🗽 New York
  • NY PFL: Up to 12 weeks job-protected paid leave; applies to all employers
  • Wage replacement: 67% of average weekly wage, capped at 67% of NY SAWW
  • 2025 max benefit: $1,177.32/week
  • Employee-funded via payroll deduction (2025 rate: 0.388%, max $354.53/yr)
  • Eligible after 20+ hrs/week for 26 consecutive weeks
  • Employers must maintain health benefits during leave
🗺️ Other States with PFL Laws
  • All have mandatory paid family leave programs: Colorado → Connecticut → Massachusetts → New Jersey → Oregon → Rhode Island → Washington → DC →
  • Benefits vary: typically 60–90% wage replacement for 6–16 weeks
  • Most are employee-funded (or shared employer/employee); cost to employer is coordination, not necessarily cash
  • Remaining 39 states: employer policy is the only paid leave available — set your bar accordingly
⚠️
Multi-state employers: If you have employees in multiple states, you need to track each state's obligations separately. An employee working remotely from WA has different entitlements than one in TX. Consider a leave administration vendor to manage this complexity.

🛡️ Short-Term Disability (STD) Insurance

STD insurance is the most cost-effective tool for funding parental leave at smaller companies. It pays a portion of salary during the medical recovery period — typically the first 6–8 weeks after birth for the birthing parent.

💡
How it works: The employer purchases a group STD policy. When an employee gives birth, the insurer pays out (typically 60–70% of salary). The employer can then top up to 100% at a much lower cost than funding the full leave themselves.

✅
Best practices beyond legal minimums: Confirm job protection in writing before leave begins. Maintain health insurance at the same cost during leave. Avoid restructuring the employee's role while on leave. Have a documented return-to-work conversation before the employee comes back.

✅ SF PPLO Compliance Steps

If you have 20+ employees and anyone working in San Francisco, the SF Paid Parental Leave Ordinance applies. Here's what you need to do.

1
Employer Confirm you're covered You're covered if you have 20+ employees worldwide and at least one employee regularly working in SF. Part-time and temporary workers count.
SF PPLO Overview →
2
Employer Update your leave policy Your written policy must reflect the PPLO requirement: supplementing CA PFL to 100% of salary (up to $2,700/wk) for up to 8 weeks of bonding leave.
OLSE PPLO Page →
3
Employer Post required notices Display the official SF PPLO notice in your SF workplace (or send electronically if remote). Available in multiple languages from the SF OLSE.
Download Notice (PDF) →
4
Employee Employee files for CA PFL The employee applies for CA PFL (bonding) through the CA EDD. This is the employee's responsibility — HR should provide guidance and the EDD link.
CA EDD PFL →
5
Both Coordinate the top-up payment Once the employee's CA PFL award letter arrives, calculate the employer top-up: (weekly salary − CA PFL benefit), capped at $2,700/wk. Pay via normal payroll.
PPLO Calculation Guide →
6
Employer Maintain health benefits Continue contributing to the employee's health insurance at the same rate during the PPLO period. Discontinuing coverage may violate CFRA/FMLA as well.
7
Employer Keep records Maintain records of PPLO payments for at least 3 years. SF OLSE may audit compliance. Document the employee's CA PFL award amount and your corresponding top-up calculation.
SF OLSE →
